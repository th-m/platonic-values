# Theories of Truth: Conversation Summary

## Core Question

Which theories of truth make claims about truth *as it actually exists* versus truth as a functional concept? And where do correspondence and coherentism fall on that spectrum?

## The Four Major Theories

### Correspondence Theory (Truth as Reality)
- **Claim:** Truth is a *real relationship* between statements and mind-independent reality. A statement is true if it corresponds to what actually is.
- **Closest to:** Plato's realm of Forms — there is something objectively *out there* to correspond to.
- **Key figures:** Aristotle (classical formulation), Plato (the cave allegory — ascending from shadows to the sun of true reality).
- **Framework position:** This is the theory the [[value-framework|5×3 Value Framework]] adopts. Correspondence sits at Mental/Spatial because truth exists *in the relationship between mind and world* — it requires something external to correspond to.

### Coherentism (Truth as Internal Consistency)
- **Claim:** A belief is true if it fits consistently within a *web of other beliefs*. There is no stepping outside the web to check against "reality itself."
- **Key distinction:** This is *not* provisional utility — it's the stronger claim that internal consistency among beliefs is *all truth could ever mean*, since we never have unmediated access to reality.
- **Limitation:** Cannot adjudicate between two internally consistent but mutually contradictory belief systems.

### Pragmatism (Truth as What Works)
- **Claim:** Truth is what produces good outcomes. Beliefs are "true" insofar as they enable flourishing, provide resilience, and generate integration.
- **Key figure:** William James — truth must be judged by its practical consequences, its "cash value."
- **Note:** This is what was initially described as coherentism ("useful utility until contradiction is found") — pragmatism is the better label for that idea.

### Deflationism (Truth as Grammar)
- **Claim:** "True" is just a grammatical convenience. Saying "it's true that snow is white" adds nothing meaningful to "snow is white."
- **Implication:** Truth isn't a substantive property at all — just a linguistic shortcut.

## The Spectrum

| Theory | What truth *is* | Ontological commitment |
|---|---|---|
| **Correspondence** | Alignment between statement and reality | Strongest — reality has a structure to match |
| **Coherentism** | Maximal consistency within a belief web | Moderate — no claim about reality itself |
| **Pragmatism** | Whatever works, produces good outcomes | Moderate — validated by consequences, not structure |
| **Deflationism** | A grammatical convenience | Weakest — truth isn't a real property |

## Key Insight for the Framework

Coherentism and correspondence aren't necessarily opposed in practice. Coherentism describes *how people navigate* truth (testing beliefs for internal consistency), while correspondence describes *what truth is* (alignment with reality). The coherentist method can serve a correspondence metaphysics — we use consistency-checking as our *epistemological process* while aiming at correspondence as the *metaphysical goal*.

This matters for the framework because Correspondence is positioned as the Spatial dimension of the Mental tier precisely because truth requires something *outside yourself* to correspond to. The claim that "'your truth' and 'my truth' language is incoherent when discussing facts" is a distinctly correspondence-realist commitment.
