*A Comprehensive Map of Human Values and Their Disorders*

# Introduction

This framework provides a systematic map of human values across all major domains of life. It synthesizes insights from ancient philosophy, modern psychology, developmental theory, and cross-cultural research to create a practical tool for understanding both virtue and dysfunction.

At its core, the framework rests on a simple but profound insight: **every sin is a value disorder**. What we call vices, sins, or psychological dysfunction are not separate categories of human experience—they are distorted relationships to legitimate human values.

# The Framework Structure

## Overview

The framework organizes 15 fundamental values into a 5×3 matrix, where:

- **Five tiers** represent hierarchical domains from physical survival to spiritual transcendence

- **Three dimensions** capture how values manifest: internally (experience), spatially (relationship), and temporally (action)

- **Each value exists on a spectrum** with deficit (vice of deficiency) ← virtue (golden mean) → excess (vice of surplus)

## Understanding the Dimensions

### Internal Dimension

Values in the internal dimension concern states and experiences within consciousness itself. These are subjective feelings, perceptions, and internal states: how you experience your body (Sustenance), how you regard others emotionally (Regard), how you evaluate yourself (Worth), your aesthetic experience (Beauty), and your sense of meaning (Meaning).

### Spatial Dimension

Values in the spatial dimension concern relationships with external reality—the world, other people, and objects outside yourself. This includes physical movement through space (Access), social connection (Belonging), self-presentation to others (Presentation), correspondence with objective truth (Correspondence), and stewardship of creation (Custodianship).

### Temporal Dimension

Values in the temporal dimension concern processes and agency that unfold over time. These involve action, change, and causation: managing danger (Risk), distributing resources and consequences (Distribution), personal development (Cultivation), exercising power to effect change (Agency), and orientation toward ultimate concerns (Ultimacy).

## Tiered Structure

The tiers are arranged in order of organizing complexity. Higher tier disorders tend to cascade downward, corrupting subordinate values. When meaning collapses (Tier 5), agency, worth, connection, and physical care all lose their organizing purpose. Conversely, lower tier deficits create difficulty for higher tier function but don't prevent it entirely—concentration camp survivors maintained meaning despite physical deprivation, social isolation, and stripped agency.

## Omission and Commission: The Dual Modes of Value Violation

The 5×3 Value Framework recognizes that both virtues and vices can manifest through two distinct modes: omission (failure to act, passive violation) and commission (wrongful action, active violation). This distinction, deeply rooted in moral theology and philosophy, reveals that the same value can be corrupted in multiple ways.

Consider the Truth/Correspondence value at Tier 4. The deficit pole encompasses both ignorance (omission—failing to seek or know truth) and deceit (commission—actively misrepresenting reality). Both violate the virtue of Honesty, but through opposite mechanisms: one through not-doing, the other through doing. Similarly, at Tier 2, the Regard value's deficit includes both indifference (omission—not caring about others' welfare) and cruelty (commission—actively causing harm). The person who walks past someone suffering and the person who inflicts suffering both fail at compassion, though their modes differ fundamentally.

This pattern appears throughout the framework. At Tier 5, meaninglessness manifests as [[ideation/philosophy/virtues/acedia-despair-indifference|acedia]] (omission—not creating meaning) or active nihilism (commission—destroying others' meaning). At Tier 3, self-neglect appears as shame (omission—failing to value oneself) or active self-sabotage (commission—deliberately harming oneself). The framework acknowledges that within each pole—whether deficit or excess—violations can occur through either passive failure or active transgression. A person can fail to care for their body through neglect (omission) or through deliberate self-harm (commission); both are disorders of Physical/Sustenance, but they require different interventions. Understanding whether a value disorder stems from omission or commission is crucial for healing: omission requires activation and engagement, while commission requires restraint and redirection. The framework thus maps not only what values are corrupted, but how they're corrupted—revealing the full landscape of human moral and psychological struggle.

### TIER 1: PHYSICAL

**Sustenance: Neglect ← Sustenance → Gluttony**

*Deficit (Neglect):*

- Omission: Not eating, forgetting meals, passive malnutrition

- Commission: Self-starvation, deliberately depriving self (anorexia), active self-harm

*Excess (Gluttony):*

- Omission: Not stopping when full, mindless overeating

- Commission: Binge eating, actively pursuing excess food

**Safety: Recklessness ← Safety → Paranoia**

*Deficit (Recklessness):*

- Omission: Not taking precautions, ignoring obvious dangers

- Commission: Actively seeking danger, deliberately risky behavior

*Excess (Paranoia):*

- Omission: Not leaving house, passive avoidance of all risk

- Commission: Installing 20 locks, actively building bunkers, obsessive safety measures

**Rest: Exhaustion ← Rest → Sloth**

*Deficit (Exhaustion):*

- Omission: Not resting, ignoring fatigue signals

- Commission: Using stimulants, actively preventing rest

*Excess (Sloth):*

- Omission: Not getting up, passive inactivity

- Commission: Actively avoiding responsibility, deliberate laziness

### TIER 2: OTHERS

**Regard: Indifference/Cruelty ← Compassion → Self-Sacrifice**

*Deficit (Indifference/Cruelty):*

- Omission: Not caring, walking past suffering (indifference)

- Commission: Actively harming, torture, cruelty

*Excess (Self-Sacrifice):*

- Omission: Not setting boundaries, failing to care for self

- Commission: Actively martyring self, deliberate self-destruction for others

**Belonging: Isolation ← Belonging → Enmeshment**

*Deficit (Isolation):*

- Omission: Not connecting, avoiding social opportunities

- Commission: Actively pushing people away, burning bridges

*Excess (Enmeshment):*

- Omission: Not maintaining boundaries, losing self in group

- Commission: Actively forcing connection, stalking, possessive behavior

**Justice: Injustice ← Justice → Vengeance**

*Deficit (Injustice):*

- Omission: Not addressing wrongs, ignoring injustice

- Commission: Actively perpetrating injustice, oppression

*Excess (Vengeance):*

- Omission: Not forgiving, holding endless grudges passively

- Commission: Actively pursuing revenge, vigilantism

### TIER 3: SELF

**Worth: Shame ← Dignity → Pride**

*Deficit (Shame):*

- Omission: Not valuing self, failing to acknowledge worth (Aquinas's pusillanimity)

- Commission: Actively degrading self, self-sabotage, self-hatred

*Excess (Pride):*

- Omission: Not recognizing limits, ignoring flaws

- Commission: Actively putting self above others, arrogant displays

**Presentation: Hiding ← Authenticity → Exposure**

*Deficit (Hiding):*

- Omission: Not showing true self, passive concealment

- Commission: Actively creating false persona, catfishing

*Excess (Exposure):*

- Omission: No filter, not maintaining privacy

- Commission: Actively oversharing, forcing inappropriate intimacy

**Cultivation: Stagnation ← Growth → Perfectionism**

*Deficit (Stagnation):*

- Omission: Not learning, avoiding growth opportunities

- Commission: Actively resisting change, sabotaging development

*Excess (Perfectionism):*

- Omission: Not accepting current self, never satisfied with progress

- Commission: Actively punishing self for imperfection, obsessive improvement

### TIER 4: MENTAL

**Beauty: Ugliness ← Beauty → Aestheticism**

*Deficit (Ugliness):*

- Omission: Not appreciating beauty, aesthetic blindness

- Commission: Actively creating ugliness, vandalism, destruction of beauty

*Excess (Aestheticism):*

- Omission: Not seeing beyond surface, missing substance

- Commission: Actively sacrificing other values for beauty (starving for appearance, etc.)

**Correspondence: Ignorance/Deceit ← Honesty → Brutality**

*Deficit (Ignorance/Deceit):*

- Omission: Not knowing, failing to seek truth (ignorance)

- Commission: Actively lying, deceiving (deceit)

*Excess (Brutality):*

- Omission: Not tempering truth with compassion, tactless

- Commission: Actively using truth as weapon, cruel honesty

**Agency: Helplessness ← Agency → Control**

*Deficit (Helplessness):*

- Omission: Not acting, passive victim mentality

- Commission: Actively giving up power, learned helplessness

*Excess (Control):*

- Omission: Not allowing others autonomy, micromanaging passively

- Commission: Actively dominating, tyranny

### TIER 5: SPIRIT

**Meaning: Acedia ← Purpose → Fanaticism**

*Deficit (Acedia):*

- Omission: Not creating meaning, existential passivity (acedia proper)

- Commission: Actively destroying meaning, nihilism, denying all purpose

*Excess (Fanaticism):*

- Omission: Not questioning meaning, blind faith

- Commission: Actively forcing meaning on others, proselytizing, fundamentalism

**Transcendence: Profanity ← Sacred → Religiosity**

*Deficit (Profanity):*

- Omission: Not recognizing sacred, materialism

- Commission: Actively desecrating, blasphemy, iconoclasm

*Excess (Religiosity):*

- Omission: Not engaging ordinary life, otherworldliness

- Commission: Actively imposing religion, theocracy, religious tyranny

**Unity: Fragmentation ← Integration → Dissolution**

*Deficit (Fragmentation):*

- Omission: Not integrating parts of self, passive compartmentalization

- Commission: Actively fragmenting self, dissociation, splitting

*Excess (Dissolution):*

- Omission: Not maintaining boundaries, losing self passively

- Commission: Actively dissolving self, pathological ego death

# Theoretical Foundations

The framework synthesizes multiple schools of thought into a coherent whole. Each influence contributes essential elements:

## Aristotelian Virtue Ethics

Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (350 BCE) established the foundational principle: every virtue is a mean between two vices. Courage stands between cowardice and recklessness. Generosity between stinginess and profligacy. This framework extends that insight systematically across all value domains.

## Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

The five tiers directly parallel Maslow's developmental stages: Physical corresponds to physiological and safety needs; Others to love and belonging; Self to esteem; Mental to cognitive and aesthetic needs; Spirit to self-actualization and self-transcendence. This grounding ensures the framework addresses the full range of human motivations.

## Haidt's Moral Foundations

Jonathan Haidt identified six innate moral foundations across cultures: Care/Harm (mapped to Regard), Fairness/Cheating (Distribution), Loyalty/Betrayal (Belonging), Authority/Subversion (Agency), Sanctity/Degradation (Custodianship), and Liberty/Oppression (Access). The framework integrates these empirically-validated moral intuitions.

## Schwartz's Universal Values

Shalom Schwartz's cross-cultural research identified ten values found universally: Self-Direction (Agency/Cultivation), Stimulation (Beauty), Hedonism (Sustenance), Achievement (Cultivation), Power (Agency), Security (Risk), Conformity (Belonging), Tradition (Custodianship), Benevolence (Regard), and Universalism (Distribution). This ensures the framework reflects actual human values across cultures.

## Jungian Psychology

Carl Jung's concept of the shadow—the repressed, denied aspects of personality—maps perfectly onto the deficit and excess poles of each value. Shadow work involves recognizing where we've abandoned the golden mean and integrating both poles of experience.

## Christian Theology

Thomas Aquinas's *Summa Theologica* (1265-1274) Christianized Aristotle's virtue ethics, systematically analyzing sins as deviations from virtue. The seven deadly sins map cleanly: Pride (excess Worth), Greed/Gluttony (excess Sustenance), Lust (excess Sustenance), Wrath (excess Agency), Sloth (deficit Agency), Envy (deficit Worth).

# Application to Shadow Work

## Core Principle: Values as Portfolio

Shadow work, in this framework, is **value portfolio rebalancing**. Just as a financial portfolio can be overweighted in some assets and underweighted in others, your value portfolio has imbalances. The goal is not to maximize every value, but to find your personal golden mean for each.

## Diagnostic Process

**Step 1: Identify Your Shadows**

Review each of the 15 values. Where do you consistently fall into deficit or excess? Common patterns:

- Do you sacrifice Sustenance (physical needs) for Cultivation (achievement)?

- Does your Worth depend entirely on others' Regard (external validation)?

- Do you swing between Helplessness and Tyranny in Agency, unable to find collaborative power?

- Have you abandoned Meaning entirely (despair) or clung to it rigidly (fanaticism)?

**Step 2: Recognize the Legitimate Value**

Every shadow points to a neglected value. The workaholic in excess Cultivation is actually seeking legitimate Development. The people-pleaser in excess Regard actually needs healthy Compassion. The shame-filled person in deficit Worth deserves authentic Dignity. Name the underlying value being distorted.

**Step 3: Explore the Opposite Pole**

Jung taught that integration requires facing the opposite. If you're stuck in excess (Pride), you must explore the deficit (Shame). If you're trapped in deficit (Helplessness), you must taste the excess (Tyranny). Only by consciously experiencing both poles can you find the center.

**Step 4: Find Your Personal Mean**

The golden mean is not an abstract universal—it's personal and contextual. Your ideal point on the Risk spectrum (Recklessness ← Prudence → Fear) may differ from mine based on temperament, life circumstances, and values. Integration means finding *your* balance, not conforming to a theoretical ideal.

## Working with Specific Values

**Example: Worth (Shame ← Dignity → Pride)**

*Shadow of Deficit (Shame):*

- Constant self-criticism and feeling fundamentally flawed

- Unable to accept compliments or acknowledge accomplishments

- Hiding authentic self for fear of rejection

*Shadow of Excess (Pride):*

- Narcissistic need for admiration and superiority

- Inability to acknowledge mistakes or limitations

- Defensive aggression when worth is questioned

*Integrated Virtue (Dignity):*

- Stable sense of inherent worth independent of performance

- Able to acknowledge both strengths and weaknesses

- Self-respect without arrogance, humility without self-contempt

## Integration Practices

**Contemplative Inquiry**

Regularly examine your value portfolio. Use the framework as a map for meditation or journaling. Where am I out of balance? Which values have I abandoned? Which have I overinvested in? What legitimate needs are my vices attempting to meet?

**Behavioral Experiments**

Deliberately practice the neglected pole. If stuck in Isolation (deficit Belonging), schedule social connection. If trapped in Enmeshment (excess Regard), practice healthy boundaries. Small, repeated experiments gradually shift your center point.

**Therapeutic Context**

This framework works naturally with Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), and psychodynamic approaches. It provides a shared language for client and therapist to map psychological patterns and track progress in rebalancing values.

# Cross-Cultural and Religious Applications

The framework is intentionally designed to map across different moral traditions:

**Christian Tradition**

- Seven Deadly Sins and corresponding virtues

- Thomas Aquinas's virtue ethics and moral theology

**Buddhist Tradition**

- Five Precepts (ethical guidelines)

- Eightfold Path (systematic cultivation of virtue)

- Middle Way philosophy (avoiding extremes)

**Islamic Tradition**

- Major and minor sins (kabair and saghair)

- Virtues cultivated through submission to divine will

**Hindu Tradition**

- Yamas and Niyamas (ethical restraints and observances)

- Dharma as right relationship to cosmic order

**Jewish Tradition**

- 613 Mitzvot organized into positive and negative commandments

- Emphasis on right action and communal responsibility

# Conclusion

The 5×3 Value Framework offers a comprehensive map of the moral and psychological landscape. It demonstrates that seemingly disparate ethical systems—from ancient philosophy to modern psychology—describe the same underlying territory.

By recognizing that sins are value disorders and virtues are balanced relationships to legitimate values, the framework provides both diagnostic clarity and a path toward integration. Shadow work becomes not the mysterious pursuit of unconscious complexes but the practical work of value portfolio rebalancing.

Whether used for personal growth, therapeutic intervention, or interfaith dialogue, the framework provides a shared language for humanity's ancient quest: learning to live well.
