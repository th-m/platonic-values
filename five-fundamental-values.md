The Five Fundamental Values

Why All Virtues and Vices Are Expressions of Just Five Core Domains

The Central Thesis

Every virtue, vice, sin, psychological disorder, moral failing, and ethical achievement that has ever been named—from the seven deadly sins to modern psychiatric diagnoses, from Aristotelian virtues to Buddhist precepts—is ultimately an expression of one of only **five fundamental values**.

These five values represent the *exhaustive domains* of human existence. There are no other realms of life. Everything you experience, every relationship you have, every action you take, every feeling you feel—all of it occurs within these five domains:

1.  **PHYSICAL** — Your relationship to your body and material survival

2.  **OTHERS** — Your relationship to other people and social reality

3.  **SELF** — Your relationship to your own identity and development

4.  **MENTAL** — Your relationship to truth, beauty, and power

5.  **SPIRIT** — Your relationship to meaning, transcendence, and ultimate reality

What creates the seemingly infinite variety of virtues and vices is not the existence of additional fundamental domains, but rather the **dimensional and modal expressions** of these five:

- **Three dimensions:** Internal (experience), Spatial (relationship), Temporal (action)

- **Three positions:** Deficit (too little), Mean (virtue), Excess (too much)

- **Two modes:** Omission (not doing), Commission (wrongly doing)

This document demonstrates that **any virtue or vice ever named** can be mapped to one of these five fundamental values, expressed through a particular dimension, at a particular position on the spectrum. The apparent complexity of human morality reduces to elegant simplicity: five domains, three dimensions, three positions.

Why Only Five? The Exhaustiveness Argument

The Complete Map of Human Experience

Consider what it means to be human. You have:

6.  **A body** that requires sustenance, safety, and rest (PHYSICAL)

7.  **Other people** you must relate to, cooperate with, compete with, love, hate, help, or harm (OTHERS)

8.  **An identity** you develop, present, evaluate, and cultivate (SELF)

9.  **A mind** that seeks truth, appreciates beauty, and exercises agency (MENTAL)

10. **A spiritual dimension** that seeks meaning, reverence, and connection to something beyond yourself (SPIRIT)

*What else is there?* Try to name a human experience, relationship, action, or concern that doesn't fall within these five domains. You cannot. These five exhaust the possibilities:

- Everything material and biological → PHYSICAL

- Everything interpersonal and social → OTHERS

- Everything about your own identity and growth → SELF

- Everything cognitive, aesthetic, and agentic → MENTAL

- Everything about ultimate meaning and transcendence → SPIRIT

These aren't arbitrary categories—they're *ontologically necessary*. To be human is to have a body, exist among others, possess an identity, think and act, and seek meaning. There are no additional fundamental domains because there's nothing else a human being fundamentally *is* or *does*.

Why Not More? Why Not Fewer?

**Could there be a sixth fundamental value?** No, because:

- PHYSICAL covers all material/biological concerns

- OTHERS covers all interpersonal concerns

- SELF covers all identity concerns

- MENTAL covers all cognitive/aesthetic/agentic concerns

- SPIRIT covers all meaning/transcendence concerns

Any proposed sixth value would necessarily be either: (a) a subset of one of these five, or (b) a combination of multiple values, or (c) not actually a fundamental human concern.

**Could there be fewer?** Could we reduce to four or three? No, because each of these five represents an *irreducible* domain:

- You cannot reduce PHYSICAL to MENTAL—body is not reducible to mind

- You cannot reduce OTHERS to SELF—social reality is not reducible to individual identity

- You cannot reduce SPIRIT to MENTAL—meaning-making transcends cognition

- Each represents a distinct ontological category that cannot be collapsed into the others

Five is the minimum necessary and maximum sufficient number of fundamental values. It is *exactly right*.

How Dimensions Create Apparent Variety

Each fundamental value manifests through **three dimensions**, creating what appear to be different virtues and vices but are actually the same underlying value expressed differently:

11. **INTERNAL dimension:** How you experience this value subjectively (feelings, states, perceptions)

12. **SPATIAL dimension:** How you relate to this value in space (relationships, boundaries, positions)

13. **TEMPORAL dimension:** How you act on this value over time (processes, changes, causation)

Example: The fundamental value **PHYSICAL** expresses through three dimensions:

- **Internal/PHYSICAL:** SUSTENANCE (how your body feels — hunger, satiation, nourishment)

- **Spatial/PHYSICAL:** ACCESS (how you move through space — freedom, confinement, navigation)

- **Temporal/PHYSICAL:** RISK (how you manage danger over time — recklessness, prudence, fear)

These appear to be three different concerns—eating, movement, and safety. But they're all expressions of the single fundamental value: your relationship to your physical body and survival. The dimensionality creates apparent multiplicity from underlying unity.

This pattern repeats for all five fundamental values. Each manifests through three dimensions, creating 15 specific value expressions. But these 15 are not 15 *different* values—they're *the same five values* expressed through three dimensions.

How Positions Create Virtues and Vices

Each of the 15 value expressions exists on a **spectrum with three positions**:

14. **DEFICIT** — Too little of the value (the vice of deficiency)

15. **MEAN** — Balanced relationship to the value (the virtue)

16. **EXCESS** — Too much of the value (the vice of surplus)

This is Aristotle's doctrine of the mean applied systematically. Every virtue is a mean between two vices. But what creates the appearance of hundreds of different virtues and vices is that *each of the 15 expressions has its own spectrum*.

Example: SELF/Worth (Internal dimension of the Self value)

- **DEFICIT:** Shame, pusillanimity, self-hatred, low self-esteem

- **MEAN:** Dignity, magnanimity, healthy self-worth

- **EXCESS:** Pride, arrogance, narcissism, vainglory

All of these—shame, dignity, pride, magnanimity, pusillanimity, narcissism, self-hatred, arrogance—are *not different values*. They're different *positions on the same value*: your internal relationship to your own worth.

Multiply this by 15 value expressions, and you get 45 positions (15 × 3). But these 45 positions all reduce to expressions of the same five fundamental values at different points on their spectrums across three dimensions.

How Modes Create Further Variety

Within each position (deficit or excess), violations can occur through **two modes**:

17. **OMISSION** — Failing to act, passive violation, not-doing

18. **COMMISSION** — Wrongful action, active violation, wrongly-doing

This creates what appears to be even more moral categories, but again, these are *not additional values*—they're different *modes of violating the same value*.

Example: OTHERS/Regard (Internal dimension of the Others value)

**Deficit violations:**

- **Omission:** Indifference (not caring about others' suffering)

- **Commission:** Cruelty (actively causing others' suffering)

Both violate the same value (compassionate regard for others), but through opposite mechanisms. The person who walks past suffering and the person who inflicts suffering both fail at the same fundamental value, just through different modes.

This modal distinction applies throughout the framework, creating apparent complexity from underlying unity. Ignorance and deceit both violate truth (MENTAL/Correspondence). Self-neglect and self-harm both violate self-care (PHYSICAL/Sustenance). The mode differs; the fundamental value violated remains the same.
