Sins, Disorders & Temptations

*A Cross-Cultural Analysis Through the Five Fundamental Values*

Introduction

Every human culture has developed frameworks for understanding moral and psychological dysfunction. What the Christian tradition calls "sins," Buddhism calls "hindrances," modern psychology calls "disorders," and Greek philosophy called "vices." Despite different terminology, these traditions describe the same underlying territory: distorted relationships to fundamental human values.

This document maps sins, disorders, and temptations from major world traditions to the five fundamental values: Physical, Others, Self, Mental, and Spirit. By examining what each culture warned against, we can infer what they valued most.

Part IV: Historical Shifts in Value Priority

Ancient/Classical Period (Pre-500 CE)

**Primary Focus: Mental** (wisdom, knowledge, rational control)

Greeks held that ignorance is the root evil. Stoics taught mastery of passions through reason. Buddhism identified delusion as what binds; wisdom liberates.

Medieval Period (500-1500 CE)

**Primary Focus: Spirit + Self** (meaning through humility)

Christianity taught that pride is the root sin; humility is the path. Islam emphasized submission to Allah and warned against arrogance. Acedia was recognized as a spiritual crisis.

Reformation/Early Modern (1500-1800)

**Primary Focus: Self** (individual conscience, worth before God)

Protestant emphasis on individual relationship with God. Shame theology intensified. Pride focus continued without the balancing recognition of pusillanimity.

Modern Period (1800-1950)

**Primary Focus: Mental** (progress, reason, science)

The Enlightenment promised that reason conquers superstition. Psychology emerged as a field, and mental health became a category. "Disorders" began replacing "sins" in cultural vocabulary.

Postmodern/Contemporary (1950-present)

**Primary Focus: Self + Others** (identity, belonging, authenticity)

The self-esteem movement emerged. Identity politics became central. Simultaneously, a meaning crisis (Spirit deficit) has become epidemic, along with a loneliness epidemic (Others deficit).

Part V: Universal Patterns

Despite cultural variation, certain patterns appear across all traditions:

1\. Pride/Arrogance Appears Everywhere

Christian Pride, Buddhist Māna, Hindu Mada, Islamic Kibr, Greek Hubris—self-inflation is universally recognized as dangerous. Every major tradition warns against excess Self/Worth.

2\. Anger/Wrath Is Universal Concern

Christian Wrath, Buddhist Dvesha, Hindu Krodha, Islamic Ghadab—excess agency turned destructive is condemned across cultures. This represents Mental/Agency at dangerous excess.

3\. Greed/Attachment Crosses All Traditions

Christian Greed, Buddhist Rāga, Hindu Lobha, Islamic Hubb ad-dunyā—physical excess and misplaced value attachment appears in every tradition's warnings.

4\. Delusion/Ignorance as Root

Buddhist Moha, Hindu Moha, Greek Ignorance, Islamic Ghaflah—mental clarity is seen as prerequisite for all virtue. Many traditions identify delusion as the foundational problem.

5\. Meaning-Crisis Recognized (Though Named Differently)

Christian Acedia, Existentialist Nihilism, Modern Depression—Spirit deficit as civilization-level threat appears across eras under different names. The Desert Fathers saw it clearly; modern psychology is rediscovering it.

Conclusion

The remarkable convergence across traditions suggests that human beings face the same fundamental challenges regardless of culture or era. The five values—Physical, Others, Self, Mental, and Spirit—represent universal domains of human existence. Every tradition has developed language for the characteristic ways humans fall into deficit or excess in each domain.

What differs is emphasis. Eastern traditions tend to foreground Mental (delusion, clarity). Western monotheisms emphasize Spirit (relationship to transcendent) and Self (humility vs. pride). Confucianism prioritizes Others (social harmony). Modern psychology focuses on Self (identity, self-esteem) while often missing Spirit entirely.

The framework reveals that these are not contradictory emphases but complementary perspectives on the same territory. A complete understanding of human flourishing requires attention to all five values—which is precisely what the great wisdom traditions, taken together, provide.
